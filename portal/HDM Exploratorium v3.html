Octave VI · Decolonial Recovery
Mathematical thinking is not a Western invention.
It is a human one.
Ethnomathematician Paulus Gerdes documented mathematical intelligence embedded in cultures across Africa, Asia, and the Americas. His finding: the same structures — graph theory, symmetry, matrix algebra — were discovered independently. The intelligence was not transmitted. It was native.
This is the epistemological gate. If mathematics is a pan-human capacity — and the evidence says it is — then HDM is not a metaphor. It is a legitimate formalization of a deep human truth.
"Mathematics is not the product of a particular culture sphere, 'western,' but a common human experience." — Paulus Gerdes, 1994

The Star That Crossed Every Ocean
Nine cultures. Four continents. One mathematical truth. Zero contact.
In 1988, Gerdes documented a single decorative motif — a "toothed square" — found independently across the world. The weavers of the Amazon, the tile-makers of North Africa, and the basket-makers of Angola all discovered the Pythagorean Theorem through their hands — centuries before it was written in a textbook.
