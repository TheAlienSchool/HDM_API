Two factions. Four pieces each. One win condition: the BLOOM.

## PART IV :: HOW TO PLAY

```
SETUP
─────
WEAVERS   ⟁MW(01)  ⊕SG(02)  ◈HC(03)  ◉PM(04)
SHAPERS   ◈HC(09)  ⊕SG(10)  ◉PM(11)  ⟁MW(12)

One piece per face. No stacking.
Weavers move first.
```

**On your turn:** Select one of your pieces. Move it to a valid destination based on its type. If an opponent's piece occupies the destination, it is captured and removed — unless it is a Pattern Monk standing on a BLOOM face, which is immune.

A face occupied by your own piece cannot be entered.

**BLOOM:** At the end of your turn, if your faction has at least one piece on each of the four BLOOM faces (1, 6, 9, 12) simultaneously — BLOOM is achieved. The game ends. You win.

**Flow Mode (F key):** With a piece selected, press `F`. The piece automatically moves one step toward the nearest unoccupied BLOOM face, guided by the shortest path through the adjacency graph. A gift from the geometry. Use it when the pattern becomes clear and the move is obvious.

**The game is won by attention, not aggression.** A player who captures greedily loses pieces and coverage. A player who moves toward BLOOM steadily, protecting their Pattern Monks, is already ahead.

## PART V :: THE FIVE LAWS OF DODECAHEDRAL PLAY

*Derived from the Five Platform Laws. Discovered, not invented.*

---

**LAW I :: PATTERNS FIRST**

The four BLOOM faces — Seed (1), Relationship (6), Integration (9), Harvest (12) — form a cross-equatorial spine. Seed and Harvest are the poles. Relationship and Integration are the mid-belt anchors. See this pattern before moving any piece. Every opening move should be evaluated against this spine.

*The player who moves toward the pattern wins. The player who reacts to their opponent loses.*

---

**LAW II :: LIVENESS**

The board is alive. The faces tell you what they want. Shadow-work (8) draws conflict — pieces there get captured. Imagination (5) opens unexpected paths — it touches three BLOOM faces. Stewardship (10) is the quiet powerbroker. Listen to where the board is directing your pieces, not just where your strategy dictates.

*The move the board is asking for is usually better than the move you planned.*

---

**LAW III :: TRANSLATION**

A piece that cannot find a valid move is communicating something. A Pattern Monk locked on Integration, surrounded by opponent pieces, cannot move — but it doesn't need to. Immobility is not failure. It is the piece completing its function.

*The Pattern Monk that never moves may be the piece that wins the game.*

---

**LAW IV :: FRIENDLY CHAOS**

The Harmonic Cartographer is your chaos engine. Its knight-jump crosses the equator in one move. From Face 3, it reaches Face 9. From Face 11, it reaches the upper belt. Use it to disrupt opponent BLOOM buildup unexpectedly. A single Cartographer jump, timed correctly, delays an opponent's BLOOM by three to five turns.

*Controlled surprise is a form of mathematics.*

---

**LAW V :: COMFORT AS TECHNOLOGY**

Patience is a move. Holding a BLOOM face with a Signal Gardener while your opponent scrambles is as powerful as any offensive sweep. The player who rushes toward BLOOM often overextends. The player who occupies two BLOOM faces early and simply holds — breathing, watching — often wins the endgame.

*We do not play fast. We play present.*
