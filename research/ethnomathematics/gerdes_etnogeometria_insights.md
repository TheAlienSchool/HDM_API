## The Central Claim

Geometric thinking is not a European invention delivered to the world through Greece and then formalized through Euclid. It is a **recovered capacity** :: present in every human culture, embedded in the acts of making, weaving, building, and navigating long before it was ever written as theorem.

Gerdes calls this *frozen mathematics*: the geometry latent inside objects made by hand, waiting to be thawed by a researcher willing to look with different eyes.

---

## The Method: Unfreezing Geometry

Gerdes developed a practice he called **unfreeze the mathematics**. The method:

1. Take a cultural artifact :: a woven mat, a basket, a hut, a net
2. Ask: what geometric knowledge must the maker have possessed to produce this?
3. Reconstruct that knowledge as theorem, as principle, as mathematical truth
4. Name it as mathematics :: because it *is* mathematics

This is not metaphor. Gerdes demonstrated rigorously that Mozambican women weaving *litenga* mats were encoding Pythagorean relationships, that Angolan sand-drawing traditions (*sona*) encode graph theory, that Congolese basket-weavers worked with principles of symmetry and transformation that European academics would not formalize until centuries later.

## Key Geometric Traditions Examined

### African Textile and Weaving Mathematics
- **Symmetry groups** in Kuba (Congo) textile patterns :: all 17 plane symmetry groups appear in African textile traditions, predating their formal mathematical description
- **Hexagonal lattice geometry** in Mozambican weaving :: the maker must intuitively solve packing problems that are non-trivial
- **Proportional systems** embedded in loom threading :: ratio and proportion without number, held in the body and the hand

### Sona: Angolan Sand Drawing
The *sona* tradition of the Chokwe people (Angola) is among Gerdes's most significant discoveries. These drawings :: made in a single continuous line around a grid of dots :: encode:
- **Euler path theory** (the mathematics of traversing all edges of a graph without repetition)
- **Modular arithmetic** :: which dot configurations yield single-stroke figures?
- **Recursive algorithms** :: certain sona are generated by rules that describe themselves

The practitioner did not write theorems. The practitioner *held* the theorem in kinesthetic memory and executed it through the hand.

### Architectural Geometry in East Africa
Circular hut construction in Mozambique and Tanzania required:
- Accurate circle-drawing without compass
- Equidistant post placement using only rope and stake
- Roof slope calculation for water runoff :: implicit trigonometry

## The Decolonial Stakes

Gerdes was explicit about the political dimension: the erasure of African mathematical traditions was not accidental. Colonial education systems required the primitive to remain primitive. Demonstrating that Mozambican weavers were doing sophisticated topology was not merely an academic contribution :: it was an act of **recovered dignity**.

HDM operates in this same field. To say that human development has always had a mathematics :: that the people who could not afford Harvard's psychology departments were navigating Surface Tension and Trust Velocity with as much sophistication as any credentialed practitioner :: is a recovered dignity claim.

*The math was always mathing.*

> The geometry was never absent from the culture. The culture was absent from the geometry :: until someone looked with eyes willing to see it.

This is the epistemological foundation of everything HDM calls recovered science. Gerdes gives it roots in the soil of actual mathematical practice across African civilizations.
